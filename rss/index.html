Choosing the right van is a crucial step when planning to build your own mobile home.

This post will give you an overview of what's available in the EU market. We will focus on panel vans only and ignore any other potential vehicle types, such as box trucks or family vans. Maybe we'll do a vehicle class comparison in the future, but for now we'll stick to panel vans with a 3,5 ton limit or less.

Popular Panel Vans

There are a couple of different panel vans available in Europe. We will consider the most popular ones, since they are readily available on the used car market.

  • Fiat Ducato
  • Peugeot Boxer
  • Citroen Jumper
  • Mercedes Sprinter
  • VW Crafter
  • Ford Transit
  • Iveco Daily

The Ducato, the Boxer and the Jumper share the same basic characteristics, such as chassis, body, dimensions etc. Since they are all built in the Italian Sevel factory, they are often referred to as Sevel Family.

Similarly, the Mercedes Sprinter and VW Crafter used to share the same basis. However, recent models are built in separate factories, so we will compare them individually.

Other factors

There are other factors when deciding for a specific van, such as price, availability, perceived build quality, repairability, cost of ownership etc. which we will not discuss here but should nonetheless be considered when buying a van.

When it comes to the engine type, Europeans currently don't have a choice. None of the listed panel vans can be ordered with a gas engine. While some panel vans can be ordered with an electric motor, there are a number of drawbacks that need to be considered. Apart from the often mentioned range anxiety, the lack of charging infrastructure, the hefty price tag (roughly 70.000 for an Iveco Daily Electric), there is even a more fundamental problem. The weight of the batteries simply does not allow for such a van to be properly converted to a camper van under the 3.5 ton limit. Currently, electric vans are simply too heavy.

]]><![CDATA[DIY Lithium Battery Part I - Basics]]>http://twoandahalfvan.github.io/diy-lithium-battery-part-1/5c3d979be55f92e29741d1e0Tue, 15 Jan 2019 08:20:42 GMT

Building your own 12V Lithium battery can be a daunting task if you have never done it before. In this series of posts we will give you all necessary information and describe how we built our own 12V Lithium battery.

WARNING

Building a 12V battery requires knowledge about basic electricity rules and security measures when handling electricity.
If you are unsure if you meet these requirements, please buy a ready-to-use battery or consult a qualified electrician.

Before you start planning your Lithium battery build, you need to know some basic terminology:

  • BMS - Battery Management System
  • SoC - State of Charge
  • OVP - Over Voltage Protection
  • UVP - Under Voltage Protection
  • LVP - Low Voltage Protection
  • Cells
  • Balancers
  • Relays
  • Hystersis

Lets start with the BMS. The task of a BMS is to protect a battery and to make sure that it operates within the specifications of the battery manufacturer. While you can operate some kind of batteries without a BMS, you should never operate a Lithium battery without a BMS. A BMS can also contain some additional features, such as a SoC detection ("how much juice is left?"), but its core function is to protect the battery from unwanted conditions, such as OVP and UVP and unsupported temperatures.

Lithium batteries operate within a voltage range. A Lithium battery with a nominal voltage of 12V has an actual voltage of 14,4V when fully charged and about 11,2V when it is empty. Now, these values are not static or fixed, some people like to charge their Lithium batteries up to 14,6V. This is often a topic of debate, because Lithium batteries tend to have a longer lifespan (measured in charge cycles) when they are not constantly fully charged or fully emptied. In fact, Lithium batteries have a significantly longer life expectation when they are operated around the 50% SoC.

Most 12V appliances can operate within the voltage range that a 12V nominal voltage battery provies. However, some 12V appliances may not work correctly when the system voltage is too low or too high, in which case you could add DC-DC converter, which will regulate the system voltage to constant 12V at the expense of a certain efficiency rate (such as 96%).

When you buy a charger for your battery, you must ensure that it is compatible with your battery in terms of the type of chemistry and system voltage. You should never use a lead battery charger on a lithium battery and vice versa, because these chargers have different cutoff voltages and charging methods or algorithms. A charging method or algorithm determines how a battery is charged over time.

In order to protect the Lithium battery cells from being over charged, we need Over Voltage Protection (OVP). Similarly, we need Under Voltage Protection (UVP), sometimes also referred to as Low Voltage Protection (LVP) to protect the cells from being discharged beyond it's specifications. If you charge or discharge your battery without OVP and UVP, you can permanently damage the cells, which may cause them to be unusable.

Now, every battery is made up of individual cells that are usually wired together in series. In the case of a 12V LiFePo4 battery, each of the four cells may have a voltage of 3,6V when the complete battery is fully charged (4 cells * 3,6V = 14,4V). However, in reality, these cells are likely not to have the same voltage. They are more likely to diverge slightly, as their chemical properties may differ slightly and change over time. Here is an example of what your cells voltage might look like if it is 100% charged.

Cell Voltage
1 3,59
2 3,60
3 3,61
4 3,60

Because every cell has slightly different chemical characteristics, some cells may be fully charged before other cells. In order to still reach the 14,4V, the charger keeps charging. If the fully charged cell continues to charge, it would be overcharged and thus damaged. It is the task of so called cell balancers to make sure that individual cells are charged equally. These balancers are typically mounted directly on top of the individual cell terminals. Balancing can be done by actively routing the charging current to the cells that needs it ("active balancing") or by simply converting the superfluous charging current to heat by means of a simple resistor (which may require a heat dissipation device, such as a fan or heat sink).

Cell balancers may also measure the voltage of the cell they are mounted to and signal an over voltage or under voltage to an upstream device called "relay". You can think of a relay as a remotely controlled switch, that switches an electrical connection based on the information it gets. For instance, if one cell balancer singals an under voltage situation to the relay input of a 230V inverter, the inverter may switch it's electrical connection to the battery off. Relays exist in various form factors.

Last but not least, a hysteresis function helps to detect temporary low or high voltage situations. For instance, a coffee machine has a very high electrical demand when it brews, causing a high dip in system voltage for a very short amount of time. In it's simplest form, a hysteresis function requires a certain amount of time to pass before it signals under voltage. Some systems have multiple OVP or LVP functions, allowing for instance a voltage level of 11,2V to exist for 30 seconds, but cutting off directly at 11V.
